This lab is used to gain a visual understanding of electric potential lines and electric field lines. The electric potential lines are charted for two given shapes. The Field lines indicate the shape of the electric field. We do not have an easy method to directly observe the electric field, but we can map the electric potentials (voltage) on the surface of the conducting paper. The equipotential lines formed by connecting a series of voltage points will allow us to view the electric field for a given shape.

1. Part 1: Electrical Equipment

1.1. Part 1.1: Power Supply

You are supplied a variable power supply to use for lab experiments. These power supplies are fairly safe for use around humans, but you can trick them into supplying a dangerous amount of power. Please use caution around the power supply and know that it is pumping out electrons which can hurt you if abused.

These power supplies have positive and negative terminals to which connect with banana plugs. This lab will only use the DC current (AC current comes later in the course).

1.2. Part 1.2: Multi-Meter

A multi-meter is the swiss-army knife for basic electronic circuits. It is not the most advanced device, but can be used to measure many useful things like voltage, current resistance, polarity, capacitance.

There are a few key points to keep in mind when using a multimeter. The most important of which has to deal with the port at the bottom of the device. One of these ports is labeled COM and should always have the black wire connected to it. The other ports measure different things, one measures amperage (A) and the other measures everything else, including voltage and resistance. Be sure to plug into the correct port for the measurement you are attempting to make.

2. Part 2: Mapping the Electric Potential

You will now map the electric potential for a couple of interesting shapes: circular charges and charged parallel plates. Please follow the following procedure on both pieces of conductive paper:

  1. The metallic pins will remain in place for the entire measurement. The multimeter will have COM connected to ground and use the positive probe to make voltage measurements.
  2. Set the voltmeter to read voltage and turn on the power supply.
  3. Adjust the voltage setting on the power supply until the voltage reads 12v.
  4. After connecting the multimeter to ground, use the red lead of the multimeter to find 10v on the paper by lightly tracing the probe across the surface of the paper. Mark a dozen or so different measurements of 10v on the graphing paper. These measurements should fall along a line. Make a sketch this 10V equipotential line on the graphing paper.
  5. Repeat step 4 for 8V, 6V, 4V, and 2V. The Equipotential lines will loosely follow the shape of the metal outlines.
  6. Equipotential lines are perpendicular to the electric field lines. Start at the positive terminal and trace a single line to the negative terminal. Make sure to draw each segment of the line perpendicular to the electric potential line. Draw enough field lines to capture the general shape of the field around the metal outlines.
  7. Repeat steps 1-7 for the second shape.
