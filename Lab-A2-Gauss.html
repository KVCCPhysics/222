1. Part 1: Calculate the Electric Force

The first half of the lab outlines an algorithmic approach to calculation of electric force from point charges. At the end of this section you will also create a Electric Field stream plot for your three charge system.

Angle convention: The angle \(\theta_{12}\) is found in the following way: start at charge 1 (first subscript) and point towards charge 2 (second subscript). \(\theta_{12}\) is the angle between the rightward direction (\(0^\circ\)) and the direction of charge 2. This angle can be any value between \(0^\circ\) and \(360^\circ\). This is the way that the unit circle is defined and how sine and cosine are defined.

Subscript convention (by-on): You will often need to identify force pairs between two objects on free body diagrams. When two charges interact, there is a force of charge 1 on charge 2, and also a force of charge 2 on charge 1 (Newtons 3rd law, action-reaction). \(F_{12}\) is the Force on charge 1 by charge 2. This force should be drawn on the free body diagram for the object with the first subscript. This can be remembered with the phrase “force by 1 on 2”.

Free Body Diagrams: It is good practice to create a Free Body Diagrams (FBD) of the Coulomb force on each of the charges. Remember the following rules: Forces have a magnitude and direction, which are displayed length and direction of an arrow on the FBD. \(r\) is the straight-line distance between the charges. Magnitude of coulomb force: \(F = k_e\frac{q~Q}{r^2}\). Direction of coulomb force: like charge repels, opposite attracts.

2. Part 2: Calculate the Electric Field

You may want to create a picture the electric field due to a set of charges (think of the dipole ). This field is a mathematical abstraction to show the direction of the force which would act on a hypothetical charge. Lets see how to calculate the E-Field

\[ \vec{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{q}{|\vec{r}|^2}\hat{r}\]

Compare this equation to the Force equation above. They are almost exactly the same. The only difference is the E-Field equation is missing the second charge. This means we will not be able to calculate the force at a charge anymore, instead we will calculate the field at an arbitrary point \(P\).

2.2. Part 2B: Shifting the origin

For a computer calculation, it is useful to move the origin to the point P which is located at point x0 in the x-dir and y0 in the y-dir. Lets define a new vector \(\vec{r}_0\) (r-naught) which encodes this shift of coordinates:

\[r_0 = (x_0,y_0)\]

This coordinate shift creates a useful equation which can calculate the electric field due to a point charge at any point in space

\[\vec{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{q}{|\vec{r}-\vec{r}_0|^3} (\vec{r}-\vec{r_0})\]

Lets use this formula to calculate the E filed at your point P. Start with the vectors:

\[\vec{r}_1-\vec{r}_0 = (x_1,y_1) - (P_x,P_y) =\] \[\vec{r}_2-\vec{r}_0 = (x_2,y_2) - (P_x,P_y) =\] \[\vec{r}_3-\vec{r}_0 = (x_3,y_3) - (P_x,P_y) =\]

Then compute the magnitude cubed of each vector:

\[|\vec{r}_1-\vec{r}_0|^3 = \left[ (x_1-x_0)^2 + (y_1-y_0)^2 \right]^{3/2}=\] \[|\vec{r}_2-\vec{r}_0|^3 = \left[ (x_2-x_0)^2 + (y_2-y_0)^2 \right]^{3/2}=\] \[|\vec{r}_3-\vec{r}_0|^3 = \left[ (x_3-x_0)^2 + (y_3-y_0)^2 \right]^{3/2}=\]

Finally you have to sum up all of these parts carefully with the E-Field equation. Here the summation is allowed because of superposition

\[\vec{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \left( \frac{q_1}{|\vec{r}_1-\vec{r}_0|^3} (\vec{r}_1-\vec{r_0}) + \frac{q_2}{|\vec{r}_2-\vec{r}_0|^3} (\vec{r}_2-\vec{r_0}) + \frac{q_3}{|\vec{r}_3-\vec{r}_0|^3} (\vec{r}_3-\vec{r_0}) \right)\]

You have all these parts, plug them all in and calculate the E field at your chosen point P

Part 1.2: Using a program to calculate the Electric Field

Wowza, that is a tedious calculation, but tedious is great for a computer! Lets write a program to do the tedious parts 

import numpy as np

# calculates the EField at point (x,y) due to a charge q located at (x0,y0)
# define the electric field for one charge, use superposition for many
def Efield(x,y,x0,y0,q):
    return 9e9*q/((x-x0)**2+(y-y0)**2)**(1.5)*np.array([x-x0,y-y0])

# Calculate Efield at (1,1) from a 1nC charge located at (0,0)
print("E(1,1)=",Efield(1.0,1.0,0.0,0.0,1e-9))

2.3. Part 3: Examining Gauss’ Law

In the next part of this lab, you will think through the the concept of electric flux, and try to build your intuition around Gauss’ Law (closed surface containing charge). The electric field could be measured using a Gauss Meter (or qualitatively with electroscope), but an accurate measurement requires somewhat extreme isolation (instead, it is easier to measure Electric Potential, but that is saved for the next lab).

Electric Field (E): Field which surrounds electrically charged objects. The field points from positive charge to negative charge.

Electric Flux (\(\Phi_e\)): The amount of field (imagined as the number of field lines) which passes through a closed surface. Flux is positive if the field exits the closed surface, and negative if the field enters the closed surface.

Gauss’ Law: The electric flux (\(\Phi_E\)) passing through a surface is proportional to the charge which is enclosed by the surface

\[\Phi_E = \int \vec{E} \cdot \hat{n} dA = \frac{q_{in}}{\varepsilon_0} \qquad \qquad \qquad \Phi_E = E \, A \, \cos(\theta) = \frac{q_{in}}{\varepsilon_0}\]
