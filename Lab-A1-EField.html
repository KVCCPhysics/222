In this lab, you will explore the reaction of small masses to electric charge. You will use a VanDeGraff generator to charge a set of foam balls, You will then use mechanical forces (tension and weight) approximate the Electric force felt because of the excess charge.

1. Part 1: Static electricity

Lets start with a warmup. Try to take some fur (please be careful, its real fur) and a PVC tube and rub together to separate charge via friction. Then use the pith balls to mess around with your electric wand. Try to see if you can make the foam attract and then repel from the rod.

Pay particular attention to where the charge is allowed to go in/out to ground. The electrons cannot simply vanish (charge is conserved) and must have a path to escape. Notice that any contact with a non-isolated surface alters the charge state, often in dramatic fashion.

2. Part 2: Charging a pith ball

Required materials:

  • Two identical pith balls
  • Non-conductive threads or fine filaments for suspending the pith balls
  • A Van de Graaff generator (or a charged Leyden jar)
  • Insulating stand or support arm
  • Protractor for angle measurements
  • Tips:
    • Do not touch the pith balls with your hands after charging. This will discharge the electrons to ground.
    • If charge leaks away too quickly, try reducing humidity, using a smoother pith ball, or reapply graphite.

2.1. Procedure to charge the pith ball

  1. Set up the workspace by hanging each pith ball from a non-conductive thread so they are side-by-side, but not touching. Ensure the area is clean, dry, and as free as possible from drafts.
  2. The instructor will turn on the Van de Graaff generator and allow it to build up charge. Then use a Leyden jar to grab some charge from the top sphere by touching the top conductor of the jar to the top sphere of the generator. Take care to remain insulated from ground to avoid discharge.
  3. Charge the first pith ball: Bring the pith ball close to the Van de Graaff dome (or the Leyden jar’s metal electrode). Gently touch the pith ball to the charged source for a moment to transfer charge. Move it away slowly to avoid losing charge immediately.
  4. Charge the second pith ball: Repeat the same steps with the second pith ball: bring it close, touch it briefly, and move it away slowly. Both pith balls should now have a similar charge type (positive using a Van de Graaff).
  5. Observe Repulsion: The pith balls should push away from each other. If they aren’t repelling, repeat the charging steps again.

2.2. (Alternative) Procedure to charge with plastic rod:

  1. Set up the workspace by hanging each pith ball from a non-conductive thread so they are side-by-side, but not touching. Ensure the area is clean, dry, and as free as possible from drafts.
  2. Vigorously rub the plastic rod against the fur pelt for around 10 seconds. Take care to leave these charges on the insulating rod by not touching to any surfaces which are connected to ground.
  3. bring the charged rod toward the back side of a single pith ball and rotate the rod to smear the charges from all sides of the insulator on the pith ball. The pith ball should eventually pick up enough charges to repel from the insulating rod.
  4. Repeat with the second pith ball to give each ball a like charge.
  5. Observe Repulsion: The pith balls should push away from each other (If they are still not repelling, repeat the charging steps again)

Charge the pith balls to a large enough charge to observe a deflection. This may be challenging, so try charging multiple times.

3. Part 3: Visualizing the Electric Field

The simple version of Coulomb’s law tells you how to find the magnitude of the Electric Force between two charges

\[F = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\]

This type of equation (inverse square law) is extremely important for physics and engineering as it describes the two main forces which humans interact with on a daily basis (gravity and static electric forces). This law is simple enough to imagine in its scalar form

  • \(k_e=8.9875517862 \times 10^9 [Nm^2/C^2]\) (which is easy to remember as \(k_e=9E9\))
  • \(q_1\) and \(q_2\) are the magnitude of the charge (the sign is irrelevant until we include vectors below)
  • \(1/r^2\) means the force decreases quite quickly, as the square of the distance (two times as far, four times as weak)

This equation is nice and simple: like charges repel, opposite charges attract and the force decrease with increasing distance. This simplicity makes the electric world fun to imagine, but we need to calculate things inside of specified coordinates. The vector form of the Coulomb force law is

\[ F(r) = k_e \frac{q_1\cdot q_2}{r^2}\hat{r} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^3}\vec{r}\]

Please note the difference between these two equations. The LHS has a radial unit vector (\(\hat{r}\)) which points in the direction of \(\vec{r}\). The RHS just uses the vector \(\vec{r}\) directly but then you must modify coulombs law to have \(1/r^3\). Please do not be fooled by the inverse cube, this is just a very useful math trick to avoid having to evaluate the unit vector

\[\hat{r} = \frac{\vec{r}}{|r|} = \frac{(x,y,z)}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}}\]

The final abstraction to imagine is the concept of a field. The force requires two charges, and fits directly into the world view of Free Body Diagrams from Mechanics. The concept of a field is a bit more clever. The field is a mathematical construction to pre-calculate all of the forces for any point. After calculating the Electric Field, finding the force on a charge is as simple as multiplying the E-Field by that charge. The only thing that changes from coulombs law above is that the second charge is left out (to calculate force use \(F=qE\))

\[ E(r) = k_e \frac{q}{r^2}\hat{r} = k_e \frac{q}{r^3}\vec{r}\]
